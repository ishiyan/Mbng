The Fractal Adaptive Moving Average (FRAMA) was introduced by John F. Ehlers in his article in Technical Analysis of Stocks & Commodities (Ehlers 2005), later available in free access on his MESA website (Ehlers 2006).

FRAMA is actually an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) with a variable smoothing factor {{"\\alpha_k"}} being changed with each new sample within the minimum and the maximun boundaries. {{"\\tag*{(1)}frama_{k}=\\alpha_{k} x_{k} + (1-\\alpha_{k})frama_{k-1}"}} where {{"\\alpha_{k}\\in\\left[\\alpha_{slow}, 1\\right]"}}. Here {{"\\alpha_{slow}"}}, the slowest smoothing factor, is the parameter of the indicator.

The concept of FRAMA is to relate the fractal dimension {{"\\mathcal{D}_{k}"}}, calculated on a moving window of {{"\\ell"}} samples, to the EMA smoothing factor {{"\\alpha_k"}}, thus making the EMA adaptive. {{"\\tag*{(2)}\\alpha_k=e^{\\omega(\\mathcal{D}_{k}-1)}, \\mathcal{D}_{k}\\in\\left[1,2\\right]"}} where {{"\\tag*{(3)}\\omega=\\ln(\\alpha_{slow}), \\alpha_{slow}\\in\\left(0,1\\right)"}} The default value of {{"\\alpha_{slow}"}}, recommended by Ehlers, is {{"0.01"}}. This corresponds to an equivalent smoothing length of {{"\\ell_{slow} = \\frac{2}{\\alpha_{slow}}-1=199"}} samples.

To explain why he chose the exponential function (2), Ehlers says:
"Since the prices are log-normal, it seems reasonable to use an exponential function to relate the fractal dimension to alpha." (Ehlers 2005, p82)
This is not entirely correct (prices follow a log-normal distribution if returns are normally distributed), but is a good practical simplification.

The equation (2) maps the bounded range of {{"\\mathcal{D}_{k}\\in\\left[1,2\\right]"}} into a convenient and interpretable range of {{"\\alpha_{k}\\in\\left[\\alpha{slow},1\\right]"}}.

When {{"\\mathcal{D}_{k}=1"}}, representing a smooth trending market, {{"\\alpha_{k}=\\exp(0)=1"}} and the filter reacts instantly, making the FRAMA follow price exactly: {{"frama_{k}=x_{k}"}}.

When {{"\\mathcal{D}_{k}=2"}}, representing chaotic noisy market behavior, {{"\\alpha_{k}=\\exp(\\omega)=\\alpha_{slow}"}} and the FRAMA is very slow, suppressing noise: {{"frama_{k}=\\alpha_{slow} x_{k} + (1-\\alpha_{slow})frama_{k-1}"}}.

The fractal dimension is estimated by using a "box counting" method (Falconer 2014, chapter 2). The box-counting dimension is also known as the Minkowski–Bouligand dimension, named after Hermann Minkowski and Georges Bouligand.

We could measure the fractal dimension of prices by covering the curve with a series of small boxes, but this is burdensome task. Since price samples are typically uniformly spaced, the box count can be approximated by the average slope of the price curve. This is calculated as the highest price minus the lowest price within an interval, divided by the length of that interval. The equation for the number of boxes is: {{"\\tag*{}N=\\frac{\\max(P_{1...\\ell}) - \\min(P_{1...\\ell})}{\\ell}"}} where {{"P_{1...\\ell}"}} are {{"\\ell"}} prices in the interval.

We can divide the lookback window of length {{"\\ell"}} into two half-intervals of length {{"\\ell/2"}} and calculate two box counts {{"N_{1}"}} and {{"N_{2}"}}. Next, we can calculate the box count {{"N_{3}"}} for the whole interval {{"\\ell"}} and calculate the fractal box-counting dimension {{"\\mathcal{D}"}}. {{"\\tag*{}N_{1}=\\frac{\\max(P_{1...\\ell/2}) - \\min(P_{1...\\ell/2})}{\\ell/2}"}} {{"\\tag*{}N_{2}=\\frac{\\max(P_{\\ell/2+1...\\ell}) - \\min(P_{\\ell/2+1...\\ell})}{\\ell/2}"}} {{"\\tag*{}N_{3}=\\frac{\\max(P_{1...\\ell}) - \\min(P_{1...\\ell})}{\\ell}"}} {{"\\tag*{}\\mathcal{D}=\\frac{\\log(N_{1}+N_{2})-\\log N_{3}}{\\log 2}"}} How Ehlers came up with this formula for {{"\\mathcal{D}"}}?

According to (Falconer 2014, p. 28, equation 2.4), the box counting dimension {{"\\tag*{}\\mathcal{D}_{P}=\\lim_{\\delta\\to 0}\\frac{\\log N_{\\delta}(P)}{-\\log(\\delta)}"}} where {{"N_{\\delta}(P)"}} is the number of boxes of size {{"\\delta"}} needed to cover the price set {{"P"}}. As {{"\\delta\\to 0"}}, we are zooming in infinitely, seeing more and more details of true fractals (like the Mandelbrot set, coastline of Britain, etc.), which exhibit self-similarity at all scales.

In parctice, we only have finite-resolution data (fixed-length lookback window in this case), so we cannot directly use the limit formula above. Instead of computing the limit, we do a discrete aapproximation of the limit using two scales, full window range {{"\\ell"}} and half window range {{"\\ell/2"}}. That gives {{"\\tag*{}\\mathcal{D}\\approx\\frac{\\log N\\left(\\ell/2\\right) - \\log N\\left(\\ell\\right)}{\\log\\left(1/\\left(\\ell/2\\right)\\right) - \\log\\left(1/\\ell\\right)}"}} {{"\\tag*{}=\\frac{\\log\\left(\\frac{N\\left(\\ell/2\\right)}{N\\left(\\ell\\right)}\\right)}{\\log\\left(2/\\ell\\right) - \\log\\left(1/\\ell\\right)}"}} {{"\\tag*{}=\\frac{\\log\\left(\\frac{N\\left(\\ell/2\\right)}{N\\left(\\ell\\right)}\\right)}{\\log 2}"}} Recalling that {{"N\\left(\\ell/2\\right) = N_{1}+N_{2}"}} and {{"N\\left(\\ell\\right) = N_{3}"}}, we get {{"\\tag*{}\\mathcal{D}\\approx\\frac{\\log\\left(\\frac{N_{1}+N_{2}}{N_{3}}\\right)}{\\log 2}"}} {{"\\tag*{(4)}=\\frac{\\log\\left(N_{1}+N_{2}\\right)-\\log N_{3}}{\\log 2}"}} which is the formula Ehlers uses to calculate the box-counting fractal dimension {{"\\mathcal{D}"}}.

To summarize all this, the FRAMA is calculated as follows. The input parameters are:
  • {{"\\ell"}} - the box-counting fractal dimension period. It should be an even integre, since we have to divide the period into two half-intervals. The default value is {{"16"}} samples.
  • {{"\\alpha_{slow}"}} - the lowest value to limit {{"\\alpha"}} calculated from the box-counting fractal dimension. The default value of {{"\\alpha_{slow}"}}, recommended by Ehlers, is {{"0.01"}}. This corresponds to an equivalent smoothing length of {{"\\ell_{slow} = \\frac{2}{\\alpha_{slow}}-1=199"}} samples.
Given the next sample {{"x_k"}} and the previous FRAMA value {{"frama_{k-1}"}},
  • update the lookback window of {{"\\ell"}} samples.
  • calculate the box-counting fractal dimension {{"\\mathcal{D}"}} using (4),
  • calculate the smoothing factor {{"\\alpha"}} using (3) and (2),
  • calculate the next {{"frama_{k}"}} value using (1).

The FRAMA indicator is not primed during the first {{"\\ell-1"}} samples. You can see the full implementation of the FRAMA in Golang and in Typescript on Github.

From the digital signal processing (DSP) point of view, the FRAMA is an infinite impulse response (IIR) filter since all previous input samples contribute to its value.

Step response

Since FRAMA is an exponential moving average, its step response should be similar to the step response of the EMA.

The first two figures show the step responces of the FRAMA with the different lengths {{"\\ell"}}. We can see that the responses almost overlap, showing no dependency on {{"\\ell"}}.

This is intuitively clear because the generated step data has the constant (not random) high and low values, so the fractal dimension (volatility) remains almost the same regardless of the length {{"\\ell"}} when the indicator window moves over the step.

The two next figures show the step responces of the FRAMA with the different slowest smoothing factors {{"\\alpha_{slowest}"}}.

Since the {{"\\alpha_{slowest}"}} truncates the smoothing factor of the EMA from the lowest (slowest) side, the step response is more responsive to the step data when the {{"\\alpha_{slowest}"}} is smaller, which is the same behavior the EMA has.

Frequency response

The figures below show an amplitude and a phase lag of the unit sample response of the FRAMA as a function of a period of various signal frequencies. We cannot derive its frequency response analytically because of the adaptive nature of the FRAMA.

A period is a duration of a cycle in samples. The smallest possible period of a cycle is {{"2"}} samples. To understand this, imagine a cycle of a sinusoid which starts at zero, goes up and peaks at {{"1"}}, continues down and bottoms at {{"-1"}}, and then returns back to zero. We need at least two samples (peak and through) to represent a cycle. See more details in the {{froaiNote.title}}.

The same charts can be represented as a function of a cycle's frequency. A period ({{"\\tau"}}) is an inverse of the cycle's frequency ({{"\\nu"}}): {{"\\tau = \\frac{1}{\\nu}"}}. The smallest period {{"\\tau = 2"}} corresponds to the Nyquist frequency {{"\\nu = \\frac{1}{\\tau} = \\frac{1}{2}"}}which is the highest frequency possible in a signal. Below we use the normalized frequency which has the value of {{"1"}} at the Nyquist frequency. That is, {{"0"}} corresponds to the infinite {{"\\tau"}}, {{"1"}} corresponds to the {{"\\tau = 2"}}.
