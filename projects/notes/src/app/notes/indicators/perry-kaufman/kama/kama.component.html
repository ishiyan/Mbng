The Kaufman Adaptive Moving Average (KAMA) was introduced by Perry J. Kaufman in his book Smarter Trading (Kaufman 1995, chapter 8, pp.129-153) and later popularized in Trading Systems and Methods (Kaufman 2013, chapter 17, pp. 779-783) and in his article in Technical Analysis of Stocks & Commodities (Kaufman 2020, 38(11)).

KAMA is actually an Exponential Moving Average (EMA) with a variable smoothing factor {{"\\alpha_k"}} being changed with each new sample within the minimum and the maximun boundaries. {{"\\tag*{(1)}kama_{k}=\\alpha_{k} x_{k} + (1-\\alpha_{k})kama_{k-1}"}}

The variable smoothing factor {{"\\alpha_k"}} reacts quickly when the market is trending strongly and smoothly, but slows down and filters out noise when the market is choppy or moving sideways. The value of the {{"\\alpha_k"}} depends on the "efficiency of price movement" over a specific period, which is an input parameter of the indicator. Perry Kaufman named this efficiency measure {{"er_k"}}, the Efficiency Ratio (ER).

The ER is a ratio of the absolute value of return (the net directional movement) to the sum of absolute values of the individual returns (Kaufman calls this "volatility or noise") over the certain "efficiency ratio period", {{"\\ell_{er}"}}. {{"\\tag*{(2)}er_{k}=\\frac{|x_k-x_{k-\\ell_{er}}|}{\\sum_{i=1}^{\\ell_{er}-1}|x_{k-i+1}-x_{k-i}|}"}} The ER value ranges between {{"0"}} and {{"1"}}. An ER close to {{"1"}} indicates a highly efficient trend (strong directional movement with low noise). An ER close to {{"0"}} suggests a lot of noise relative to the net price movement (sideways or choppy market).

Given the boundaries {{"[\\alpha_{slow}, \\alpha_{fast}]"}} of the adaptive smoothing factor {{"\\alpha_k"}}, Kaufman calculates {{"\\alpha_k"}} as {{"\\tag*{(3)}\\alpha_k=(er_{k}(\\alpha_{fast}-\\alpha_{slow})+\\alpha_{slow})^2"}} When the ER is high, KAMA uses a larger {{"\\alpha"}}, making it behave like a faster, more responsive moving average to follow the clear trend. When the ER is low, the {{"\\alpha"}} is small, making KAMA behave like a slower moving average, which helps to filter out the noise in choppy markets.

If we recall that the EMA smoothing factor {{"\\alpha"}} can be expressed in terms of the EMA length {{"\\ell"}} as {{"\\tag*{(4)}\\alpha=\\frac{2}{\\ell+1}, \\ell>1"}} we can specify the {{"\\alpha_{slow}"}} and {{"\\alpha_{fast}"}} using the {{"\\ell_{slow}"}} and {{"\\ell_{fast}"}}.

To summarize all this, the KAMA is calculated as follows. Input parameters are:
  • {{"\\ell_{er}"}} - the efficiency ratio period. The default value is {{"10"}} samples.
  • {{"\\ell_{fast}"}} - the fast EMA length. The default value is {{"2"}} samples.
  • {{"\\ell_{slow}"}} - the slow EMA length. The default value is {{"30"}} samples.
Given the next sample {{"x_k"}} and the previous KAMA value {{"kama_{k-1}"}},
  • calculate the efficiency ratio using (2),
  • calculate the smoothing factor using (3),
  • calculate the KAMA using (1).

The KAMA indicator is not primed during the first {{"\\ell_{er}"}} samples. You can see the full implementation of the KAMA in Golang and in Typescript on Github.

From the digital signal processing (DSP) point of view, the KAMA is an infinite impulse response (IIR) filter since all previous input samples contribute to its value.

The step response of the KAMA cannot be derived analytically.

Frequency response

The figures below show an amplitude and a phase lag of the unit sample response of the KAMA as a function of a period of various signal frequencies.

A period is a duration of a cycle in samples. The smallest possible period of a cycle is {{"2"}} samples. To understand this, imagine a cycle of a sinusoid which starts at zero, goes up and peaks at {{"1"}}, continues down and bottoms at {{"-1"}}, and then returns back to zero. We need at least two samples (peak and through) to represent a cycle. See more details in the {{froaiNote.title}}.

The same charts can be represented as a function of a cycle's frequency. A period ({{"\\tau"}}) is an inverse of the cycle's frequency ({{"\\nu"}}): {{"\\tau = \\frac{1}{\\nu}"}}. The smallest period {{"\\tau = 2"}} corresponds to the Nyquist frequency {{"\\nu = \\frac{1}{\\tau} = \\frac{1}{2}"}}which is the highest frequency possible in a signal. Below we use the normalized frequency which has the value of {{"1"}} at the Nyquist frequency. That is, {{"0"}} corresponds to the infinite {{"\\tau"}}, {{"1"}} corresponds to the {{"\\tau = 2"}}.

The frequency response of the KAMA cannot be derived analytically.
